.01 — context

Five cards.
One month.
Zero assumptions.

Procore's Safety Hub is the command center for safety managers and project stakeholders on active construction sites. As part of a broader safety intelligence initiative, five new dashboard cards were scoped ť each surfacing a different slice of site safety data across both web and mobile.

The five cards covered: incident tracking by contractor, time lost to injury, daily hazard assessment status, financial claims, and toolbox talk attendance. All five had detailed product requirements and acceptance criteria ť but none had been user-validated before design began.

Three cards were formally flagged for a research plan due to novel visualization types. After reviewing the full set, I expanded the scope to include all five ť anchoring the work in evidence before a single pixel reached development.

why self-perform research

This was an SPR (Self-Perform Research) engagement, meaning I owned both the design and the research end-to-end. No dedicated researcher ť the same person who designed the cards planned the study, recruited participants, synthesized findings, and shipped the updates.

.02 — challenge

Built to spec.
But does it work?

The core design risk wasn't whether the cards could be built ť it was whether they'd communicate the right thing once they were. All five used visualization patterns not previously validated in the Safety Hub context. Without research, the team risked shipping cards that were technically correct but practically unusable.

The most complex cases weren't bugs ť they were subtle comprehension failures that only surface when real users try to act on real data. A stacked bar chart showing "Projected Work" vs "Actual Work" is technically unambiguous. To a safety manager opening a dashboard at 7am, it wasn't.

"You should really see the graph and think about it for a moment before understanding it."

ť P7, Construction Finance, France
the research question

Three hypotheses were formed before the study launched ť covering the Daily Hazard gauge, the Claimed to Date filter behavior, and the Toolbox Talk attendance chart. All three were tested against a static Figma prototype with 12 construction professionals.

.03 — the work

Research-driven
design.

The project moved through three distinct phases: initial design from requirements, a structured unmoderated usability study, and a round of research-backed redesigns. Here's how each played out.

phase 01 / research setup

Testing before building.

The research plan defined 5 goals, 12 research questions, and 3 explicit hypotheses ť then went live on UserTesting.com with a screener filtering for active construction professionals who use dashboards weekly or more.

method

Unmoderated remote usability test. 12 participants. 20ť25 minute sessions. Static Figma prototype with think-aloud. No facilitator ť participants worked through a scenario as a Safety Manager starting their morning site review in Procore.

research plan / UserTesting setup

three hypotheses tested

H1 ť Daily Hazard Assessments

Open hazards with no path to act.

If safety managers see a high proportion of Open assessments, they will want to take immediate corrective action ť but be unable to complete it without a drill-through link.

? supported
H2 ť Claimed to Date

Quick Filter drives misread of trend %.

If the Quick Filter doesn't surface cost code or vendor as defaults, project stakeholders will misinterpret the trend percentage as a project-wide figure rather than a filtered one.

ť inconclusive
H3 ť Toolbox Talk Attendance

Total vs. Attended ť same thing?

If the stacked bar doesn't clearly differentiate Total Possible Attendees from Attended, safety officers won't be able to identify low-attendance talks without additional context.

? partially supported
card 01 / incidents per manpower log

The anchor card.

The stacked bar + trend line card for tracking incidents by contractor was the clearest, most action-driving card on the dashboard. 11 of 12 participants understood it on first pass. All 12 correctly read the trend line.

"If something is super high here, that's like an emergency ť that needs to be dealt with before anything else."

ť P1, Operations Manager, CA
decision

No structural changes to web. A card that works for 11/12 on the first pass and drives immediate action shouldn't be changed without a research-backed reason. One mobile update was added: a unified tap interaction so a single tap on any data point triggers a combined tooltip for that day ť removing the need to distinguish between two separate tap targets.

card 02 / daily hazard assessments

Users saw the problem.
But had no way out.

11 of 12 participants expressed intent to drill into open hazards ť to see who owned them, why they were still open, and what needed immediate attention. Action completion: 0 of 12. The drill-through path didn't exist.

"Next steps I would do is drill into open items ť what's high risk, who owns it, and why it's still open."

ť P2, Senior Designer, WA
design changes

The full 360ť donut was replaced with the spec's intended semicircular gauge. The center label "Project Total: 247" ť misread by 8/12 ť was renamed to "247 Assessments." "Year-to-Date" was added as a subtitle to address the absence of timeframe context raised unprompted by multiple participants. Raw counts were added alongside percentages in the legend (e.g., "42 Open (17%)").

after ť semicircular gauge / "247 Assessments"
card 03 / hours lost to injury

The indirect calculation that didn't land.

The original "Time Lost to Injury" card used a stacked bar showing Projected Work vs Actual Work ť with the gap between them representing hours lost. Only 3 of 12 participants interpreted it correctly on the first or second pass.

"Actual work vs projected work doesn't directly translate to time lost to injury. I'd expect lost hours or days due to incidents."

ť P2, Senior Designer, WA
complete redesign

The stacked bar was replaced with a single-series red bar chart showing hours lost per month directly. The card was renamed from "Time Lost to Injury" to "Hours Lost to Injury" ť making the unit explicit and removing the mismatch between title and content. "Year-to-Date: X Hours" was surfaced as a subtitle. Nothing to derive; the number is the card.

before ť "Projected Work" vs "Actual Work"
after ť direct "Hours Lost" red bar chart
cards 04 & 05 / claims due to injury + toolbox talk

Correct data.
Wrong frame.

The "Claimed to Date" card showed $924M ť a figure that caused 3 of 12 participants to disengage before evaluating the data. A separate 3 misread the card as contractor billing. The trend percentage itself was correctly interpreted by 10/12.

claims due to injury changes

Renamed "Claims Due to Injury" to make injury context explicit and eliminate the most common billing misread. Prototype value updated to a plausible $92K. A sparkline was added to the right of the primary metric ť red when claims are increasing, green when decreasing. "vs. past month" added as a comparison label.

toolbox talk attendance changes

The stacked bar was replaced with a single-series bar chart showing attendance rate (%) per talk. The primary signal ť who attended and by how much ť is now directly readable without requiring users to compare two bars or calculate a ratio. Y-axis changed from "People" to "Attended %" (0ť100%).

.05 — reflections

What worked.
What I’d change.

Expanding scope to all 5 cards was the right call.

Only 3 cards were formally required for research. Bringing all 5 revealed that even the "simple" cards carried comprehension risks ť and validated the one card (Incidents per Manpower Log) that was clearly working. Having an anchor card to benchmark against made every other finding sharper.

Owning both design and research removed friction.

In a Self-Perform Research model, the designer who built the cards also ran the study. The context switch was demanding ť but it meant findings were translated into design decisions immediately, without a handoff gap. The synthesis-to-redesign cycle happened in days, not weeks.

I'd have run an interactive prototype for Claims Due to Injury.

The static prototype prevented Quick Filter interaction ť the second hypothesis couldn't be fully tested, and came back inconclusive. The scale confusion ($924M) was a more critical finding anyway, but an interactive prototype would have surfaced both problems simultaneously and saved a follow-up round.

Mobile should have been included from the start.

This round of research covered web only. Mobile designs existed but weren't tested. A significant portion of Procore's Safety Hub usage happens in the field on mobile ť where touch targets, panning behavior, and tap tooltips all introduce new failure modes. Round 2 is scoped; it should have been round 1.
