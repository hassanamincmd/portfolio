.01 — where it started

A research brief,
not a design spec.

I was brought into this project before any designs existed. The ask was to benchmark mobile data visualization best practices and figure out how to make safety data actionable on a phone in a construction environment. That framing — research first, design second — set the tone for everything that followed.

“I don’t want to hunt for safety data. I want to open one screen, see what needs attention, and go.”
— Recurring pattern from live user interviews, Phase 1
01 — fragmented access

Safety data lived in separate corners of the product

Incidents, observations, inspections, toolbox talks — all existed as separate modules. For a safety manager arriving on site at 7am, getting the full picture meant visiting four different areas of the app before they could walk the floor.

02 — no signal surfacing

The product didn’t triage. The user had to.

There was no view that answered: what’s open, what’s trending, who are the repeat offenders. Managers were doing the mental assembly work on top of a product that should have done it for them.

03 — mobile-hostile data

Dense dashboards designed for desktops

Safety work happens on-site, on mobile, often in glare, wearing gloves, with 30 seconds to spare. The existing data views weren’t designed for those conditions. They required sustained attention the environment didn’t allow.

.02 — my role

What I actually owned.

My title was Design Lead on mobile, but the role spanned more than screens. Across twelve months and two phases, I was involved in research, product definition, cross-platform design execution, and stakeholder alignment — often all at once.

.03 — phase 1 · months 1–6

From a research brief
to a shipped hub.

Phase 1 was the foundation. Before any designs existed, I needed to understand what safety managers actually do on site, what they look for first, and what structure would let them get in and out of the app in under a minute.

Discovery

I attended the interviews to extract architecture, not just quotes.

The UX researcher ran the sessions. My job was to listen for information hierarchy signals — what do managers look for first when they arrive on site? What do they scan, what do they skip, what makes them pull out their phone mid-walk?

what I observed

Safety managers operate in triage mode in the mornings. They don’t analyze — they check status. Open items, trend direction, and top offenders were the three things every participant referenced without being prompted. That pattern became the information model for every card in the hub.

Phase 1 — Exploration — early card research board assets/safety-hub-phase1-exploration.png
Requirements

Turning interview findings into a product scope.

After interviews, I worked with the PM to translate the patterns I’d observed into requirements. We defined two core decisions: (1) consolidate all safety modules into a single scrollable hub, and (2) give each module an overview card with the metrics that actually mattered — not every available data point.

my contribution

I defined the information hierarchy for each card type based on what managers reached for first. The structure wasn’t pulled from a product backlog — it came from watching twelve safety managers describe their mornings.

Phase 1 — Iteration — card system explorations assets/safety-hub-phase1-iteration.png
Design

One hub. A card for every safety module.

I designed the main Safety Hub page — a scrollable feed where each safety module had a dedicated overview card. Each card had to answer three questions at a glance: what’s the status, what’s the trend, and where do I go if something needs attention.

what I explored

I worked through multiple structural approaches: hub-first navigation, fixed vs. customizable module order, card stacking behavior on scroll. Flows mapped the three-tap path from hub entry to individual incident — the maximum depth a manager should need to confirm something was handled.

Phase 1 — Flows — hub architecture & navigation assets/safety-hub-phase1-flows.png
Validation

Strong signal at a customer preview session.

I presented the hub and card system to customers. The response was immediate — safety managers recognized the morning briefing pattern, several asked when it would be in the product. The concept didn’t need explaining; it just made sense to them.

why it mattered

Phase 1 closing with strong customer validation meant Phase 2 could take on harder problems — richer data cards, more complex visualizations — without re-litigating whether the hub structure itself was right. We had user confidence in the foundation before building on top of it.

.04 — phase 2 · months 6–12

Designed once.
Tested. Redesigned.

The PM handed off requirements for five new dashboard data visualization cards — for both web and mobile. I took those requirements, designed the first versions, then planned and ran a usability study before a single card went to engineering. What the research surfaced made four of five cards significantly better.

I designed the study to test one question: can a construction safety manager understand what a card is showing, and what to do next, within a realistic time window? I wrote the tasks, defined success criteria, ran synthesis, and organized findings by failure pattern — not by card. That reframing is what made the insights actionable.

finding 01

Don’t make users do math.

what participants did

The “Time Lost to Injury” card displayed projected vs. actual work hours as a stacked bar. The gap between them was meant to represent lost time. Participants consistently stared at the chart without answering — they were trying to do subtraction in their head.

“I’d expect lost hours or days due to incidents — not projected vs. actual work.”

— Usability study participant
what changed

Replaced the comparison chart with a direct “hours lost per month” visualization. The math now happens in the backend. The card title was renamed to match the unit the user expected. The number became the message — no derivation required.

Hours lost — before: stacked comparison chart assets/safety-hub-phase2-hours-before.png
Hours lost — after: direct monthly chart assets/safety-hub-phase2-hours-after.png
finding 02

Seeing the problem isn’t the same as solving it.

what participants did

The hazard assessment gauge showed the volume of open hazards clearly — participants understood something was wrong. But they couldn’t do anything about it. The card gave them a status but no path forward. Labels compounded it: “project total” was interpreted differently by nearly every participant.

what changed

Changed the full-circle gauge to a semicircle (reduces visual noise, creates natural reading direction), clarified all labels to remove ambiguity, added timeframe context, and paired percentages with raw numbers. The card now tells you the status and gives you enough context to decide whether to act.

Hazard gauge — before: full donut, ambiguous labels assets/safety-hub-phase2-hazard-before.png
Hazard gauge — after: semicircle, clear labels + counts assets/safety-hub-phase2-hazard-after.png
finding 03

Right data. Wrong frame. Misread every time.

what participants did

Two separate cards had framing problems. The workers’ compensation claims card was read as contractor billing by multiple participants — the word “injury” didn’t appear in the title. The toolbox talk attendance chart required users to subtract two bars to get the attendance ratio — a calculation that nobody should be doing at 7am before walking a job site.

“This looks like it’s tracking money. Is this contractor payments?”

— Usability study participant, on the claims card
what changed

Made injury context explicit in the card name and description. Added a trend sparkline so direction is visible before any numbers are read. Replaced the dual-bar attendance chart with a single percentage bar: attended vs. total, stated plainly. No arithmetic required.

Claims card — before & after assets/safety-hub-phase2-claims.png
Attendance card — before & after assets/safety-hub-phase2-attendance.png

One card passed without changes. It became the benchmark. When I started redesigning the others, I used it as the reference point for what “clear” actually looked like — direct labelling, matched title-to-unit, a single dominant number. Every redesign was measured against it.

.05 — what this demonstrates

Skills in practice,
not in theory.

This wasn’t a sprint or a single feature. It was a sustained year of design leadership on a complex enterprise product — research-heavy, cross-platform, multi-phase, and shaped by real user behavior.

01

Research-led design, from brief to synthesis

I didn’t receive insights from a researcher and translate them. In Phase 1, I attended live interviews and extracted the patterns that shaped the architecture myself. In Phase 2, I planned and ran the study, wrote the tasks, and organized findings into three actionable failure modes that drove four redesigns. The research wasn’t a separate workstream — it was my workstream.

02

Mobile-first with real constraints, extended to web

Every Phase 1 design decision was made for field conditions: glare, gloves, 30-second windows, one-thumb use. Phase 2 extended each card to web simultaneously — different density, different layout, different interaction patterns — designed in parallel, not as an afterthought. Cross-platform here meant two genuinely different design problems, not just a resize.

03

Stakeholder collaboration and mid-project ownership

I shaped requirements with the PM in Phase 1 rather than receiving a spec — translating user interview findings into product decisions directly. In Phase 2, I received the PM’s requirements brief and ran the entire process end-to-end: concept design, study planning, synthesis, and redesign. The continuity between both phases — the card grammar, the user mental model, the design decisions — held because I had context from day one.

04

Systems thinking over screen design

The Safety Hub is an information architecture, not a feature. One scrollable hub, six-plus safety modules, a consistent card grammar, drill-through flows into existing product areas — all of it had to hold together. The card system had rules: open count, trend, top offenders, direct path forward. Those rules meant the system could scale to any module without requiring a new design decision each time.

.06 — reflections

What I’d carry forward.

Phase sequencing is a research strategy.

Testing the hub concept with users before Phase 2 began meant we weren’t building complex data visualizations on top of an unvalidated structure. That sequencing was a deliberate risk reduction move, not just a project plan. Phase 1’s validation made Phase 2’s research riskier and more interesting — we were testing harder things on a solid foundation.

Test the “safe” cards too.

Only three of the five cards were initially flagged for usability risk. I advocated to test all five. The two that seemed straightforward had the most consistent comprehension failures. And the one that passed cleanly — the one nobody was worried about — became the design benchmark everything else was measured against.

Synthesis organization is part of the design work.

Organizing findings by failure mode rather than by card was a deliberate choice. “4 of 5 cards had problems” is a stat. “Three failure modes appear across all five cards” is a design brief. The way you structure what you learned shapes what people are able to act on. Synthesis is design.

Owning a brief end-to-end is different from executing screens.

Taking the PM’s requirements for Phase 2 and running every step from there — concept design, study planning, synthesis, redesign — with no separate handoffs between research and design meant the findings went directly into decisions. There was no translation gap. That continuity is what made the redesigns specific rather than generic.
